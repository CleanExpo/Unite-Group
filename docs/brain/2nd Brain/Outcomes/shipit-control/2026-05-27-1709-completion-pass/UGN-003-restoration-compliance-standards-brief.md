## Brief
Restoration operators sit at the intersection of physical damage, occupant health, insurer documentation, and digital records. A useful RestoreAssist content lane should therefore combine three authority clusters: IICRC for water/mold restoration practice references, OSHA/EPA for worker and occupant safety context, and NIST/CISA for cyber and data-continuity risk. The business message is that report quality is no longer just admin. It is operational evidence, customer trust, insurer communication, and business continuity.

## Risk notes
- Water-damage and mold content should reference IICRC standards without presenting marketing copy as a substitute for certified professional judgement.
- Field-data and report-quality claims should avoid promising insurance acceptance, legal compliance, or guaranteed remediation outcomes.
- Cyber content for restoration operators should treat ransomware and exploited vulnerabilities as business-continuity risks, especially where job photos, reports, customer data, and claims files are stored.
- Use NIST CSF language for governance and risk framing, not as a claim that the business is certified by NIST.
- Use CISA KEV/StopRansomware as monitoring/prevention references; do not claim a tool eliminates ransomware risk.
