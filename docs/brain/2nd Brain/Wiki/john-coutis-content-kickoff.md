## YouTube — first 5 episodes

These are intentionally NOT motivational-speaker greatest-hits. They are the conversations that establish him as the ANZ trades industry spokesman. Each episode pairs JC's lived authority with a working-class trades concern.

### Episode 1 — "What I Learned From a Bloke Who Couldn't Look Me in the Eye"

**Hook (first 8 seconds):** "He was six-two, covered in tattoos, and could not look at me. I was the smallest person in the room and the only one not scared."

**Outline (200 words):** JC opens with a scene from a recent worksite-mental-health keynote: a senior site foreman, hardened by twenty years on the tools, who couldn't make eye contact with the speaker on the skateboard. Halfway through the talk, the foreman wept openly. After the talk, he told JC he'd been planning to end his life that weekend. JC turns the camera on the audience: "Every one of you knows this man. Every site has one. He won't ring Lifeline. He won't ring his GP. But he will listen to a yarn — if you tell it right." The episode is a six-minute lesson on how to start the conversation a tradie cannot start with themselves: lead with your own crack first, never theirs; use one sentence, not a speech; offer presence, not advice; and never, ever say "are you okay" — say "I'm not great today, mate, how are you holding up". Closes on JC naming the foreman (with permission) and reading the text he sent six weeks later: "Thanks for the yarn. Still here."

**Hero CTA:** "Send this to one bloke on your crew. Just one. That's the assignment."

### Episode 2 — "Why I Refuse the Word 'Inspiration'"

**Hook (first 8 seconds):** "If you call me inspirational one more time, I'm going to roll over your foot."

**Outline (200 words):** JC names the word that follows him into every introduction — *inspirational* — and explains why he refuses to wear it himself. The word, he argues, lets the audience off the hook. It frames him as the exception so the listener doesn't have to do the work. He walks through three specific scenes where calling him "inspirational" actively damaged the moment: a sales kickoff where the CEO used it as a transition into a quarterly target, a school assembly where a teacher used it to lecture a bullied kid into being grateful, and a corporate offsite where it was used as cover for a redundancy announcement the next morning. The lesson lands hard: *inspiration* is a transfer of obligation. The listener watches, claps, leaves unchanged. Real change asks: "What did this make me do tomorrow?" If the answer is nothing, the speaker was decorative. JC reframes himself: he is not inspirational — he is a man on a skateboard with a microphone. The work is yours.

**Hero CTA:** "Subscribe if you want talks that ask something of you. Don't if you want decoration."

### Episode 3 — "The OAM That Almost Didn't Happen"

**Hook (first 8 seconds):** "When the letter arrived, I thought it was a scam. So did my mum."

**Outline (200 words):** JC tells the story of the 2020 Queen's Birthday OAM — the letter from Government House, the moment of recognition, the moment of doubt. He is honest about imposter syndrome: a 25-year career, 6 million people in audiences, and his first reaction to a national honour was disbelief. He uses the story to dismantle a myth tradies carry like a tool belt: that recognition is for other people. Not for the bloke who shows up on time for fifteen years. Not for the foreman who carried the apprentice through their wedding-week meltdown. Not for the mum running the books from the kitchen table. He names by occupation the kinds of people he believes deserve recognition that the honours system won't see, and challenges the audience: nominate one person this month. Just one. He gives the URL on screen. The episode ends with the line from the citation: "for service to the community and to sport".

**Hero CTA:** "Nominate someone. honours.pmc.gov.au. Pause this video and do it now."

### Episode 4 — "What Tony Hawk Said When the Cameras Were Off"

**Hook (first 8 seconds):** "Tony Hawk asked me one question backstage. I've thought about it every day since."

**Outline (200 words):** JC tells the story of a backstage moment with Tony Hawk at a shared event. Hawk, watching JC roll up to the green room on his custom board, asked one question — not about the disability, not about the speaking, not about the inspiration. He asked: "Who taught you to drop in?" The question, JC explains, was the most respectful thing anyone has ever asked him about his life — because it assumed competence, history, and mentorship rather than tragedy. He uses the story to teach the audience how to ask a better question of the person in their life who lives with something hard. The pattern: don't ask about the hardship; ask about the craft. Ask what they got good at because of it. Ask who taught them. Ask what they teach others. The episode lands with a direct address to the tradies in the audience: the apprentice who lost a finger, the foreman with a busted back, the bloke whose wife left during the build — *who taught them to drop in*. Find that, you find the person.

**Hero CTA:** "Ask one person on your crew this week: who taught you to drop in?"
