## 13.1 Purpose

This chapter governs how Nexus communicates, presents itself, makes claims, represents its identity, enters public discourse, creates expectations, responds to uncertainty, and protects public trust.

It ensures that communication is not treated as decoration, promotion, persuasion, or output alone.

Communication is a constitutional act because it can:

* create expectations;
* influence decisions;
* transfer knowledge;
* establish commitments;
* affect reputation;
* exercise power;
* alter stakeholder behaviour;
* expose people to risk;
* strengthen or weaken trust;
* represent the identity and intentions of Nexus.

Every communication made in the name of Nexus must therefore remain aligned with:

* constitutional purpose;
* authorised representation;
* available evidence;
* actual organisational capability;
* stakeholder circumstances;
* accessibility;
* privacy;
* safety;
* lawful conduct;
* operational reality.

Communication must never manufacture a level of certainty, capability, authority, urgency, independence, success, or public support that does not exist.

---

## 13.2 Constitutional Position

Nexus communicates to expand understanding and support confident, informed action.

Communication must serve human capability rather than exploit uncertainty, fear, dependency, status, confusion, urgency, or information imbalance.

The constitutional communication sequence is:

> **Know what is true → understand who is affected → establish authority → communicate clearly → support the promise operationally → observe stakeholder response → correct error → learn and improve.**

Communication that bypasses this sequence may create constitutional, legal, reputational, operational, or stakeholder risk.

No communication objective overrides the requirement for truthfulness.

No marketing target overrides stakeholder dignity.

No brand objective overrides operational reality.

No technological capability overrides communication authority.

No publication deadline excuses unsupported claims.

## 13.4 Constitutional Definitions

### Communication

Any deliberate transmission of information, meaning, instruction, representation, expectation, request, commitment, interpretation, or identity.

### Constitutional Communication

Communication that remains consistent with Nexus purpose, principles, authority, evidence, stakeholder obligations, and operational reality.

### Brand

The accumulated stakeholder understanding of who Nexus is, what it stands for, how it behaves, and whether its promises can be trusted.

A logo, colour system, name, slogan, visual style, campaign, or design system may express the brand but does not constitute the brand by itself.

### Representation

Any act through which a person, agent, partner, publication, platform, product, or artefact speaks, appears to speak, or may reasonably be understood as speaking on behalf of Nexus.

### Public Claim

A statement, implication, representation, comparison, forecast, promise, image, demonstration, statistic, testimonial, or omission capable of influencing stakeholder understanding or action.

### Public Commitment

A communicated promise that creates a reasonable expectation that Nexus will perform, deliver, maintain, investigate, correct, disclose, support, or avoid something.

### Claim Owner

The human role accountable for the accuracy, evidence, continued validity, conditions, and withdrawal of a claim.

### Communication Authority

Explicit permission to communicate within a defined subject, channel, audience, jurisdiction, claim class, risk level, and period.

### Brand Inheritance

The controlled process through which a Nexus unit, product, project, campaign, agent, partner, or subsidiary receives permission to express constitutional identity.

### Promise–Experience Parity

The condition in which the stakeholder experience can reasonably support the promise, representation, or expectation created by communication.

### Constitutional Brand Drift

A material divergence between:

* declared identity and actual conduct;
* public promise and delivered experience;
* constitutional purpose and promotional positioning;
* authorised brand expression and uncontrolled representation.

### Misinformation

False or materially inaccurate information communicated without necessarily intending to deceive.

### Disinformation

False or manipulated information knowingly created or distributed to deceive.

### Material Omission

The exclusion, concealment, minimisation, or obscuring of information that a reasonable stakeholder would need to interpret a communication properly.

### Synthetic Media

Text, audio, imagery, video, likenesses, voices, environments, demonstrations, or other media generated or materially altered by artificial intelligence or automated systems.

4. Marketing remains truthful, distinguishable, and non-manipulative.
5. Brand expression remains consistent with constitutional identity.

11. Public commitments remain owned, resourced, monitored, and reviewable.
12. Stakeholders have practical methods to question, challenge, complain, appeal, and seek correction.

14. Constitutional brand drift is detectable and correctable.
15. Public trust is treated as an outcome of trustworthy conduct rather than a promotional metric.

## 13.6 Governing Principles

### 13.6.1 Truth Before Persuasion

Communication must not trade accuracy for attention, engagement, conversion, urgency, simplicity, or emotional impact.

### 13.6.2 Evidence Before Assertion

Material claims must be supported by evidence proportionate to their importance, specificity, risk, and potential stakeholder effect.

### 13.6.3 Experience Before Promise

Nexus must not communicate a promise that its current authorised delivery system cannot reasonably support.

### 13.6.4 Clarity Before Impression

The likely understanding created by the entire communication governs its validity, not merely the literal accuracy of isolated words.

### 13.6.5 Authority Before Representation

No person, system, agent, partner, or channel may represent Nexus merely because it possesses information or technical publishing capability.

### 13.6.6 Accessibility Before Convenience

Communication must be designed for stakeholder comprehension rather than organisational convenience alone.

### 13.6.7 Transparency Before Ambiguity

Material sponsorship, automation, conflicts, limitations, conditions, risks, uncertainty, and commercial intent must not be disguised.

### 13.6.8 Dignity Before Exploitation

Communication must not exploit vulnerability, grief, fear, confusion, urgency, limited literacy, cultural difference, disability, power imbalance, or lack of technical knowledge.

### 13.6.9 Correction Before Reputation Protection

When material error occurs, protecting stakeholders and restoring the record take priority over avoiding embarrassment.

### 13.6.10 Stewardship Before Virality

Reach, engagement, attention, and growth are subordinate to truth, safety, stakeholder value, and long-term trust.

### 13.6.11 Human Accountability for Machine Output

Artificial intelligence may assist communication but cannot own authority, responsibility, consent, evidence, or consequences.

### 13.6.12 Consistency Without Artificial Uniformity

Nexus communications may adapt to different audiences, brands, cultures, channels, and learning needs, but must not contradict constitutional identity.

Communication representing Nexus as an organisation, including:

### Class D — Commercial and Marketing Communication

Communication intended to:

* create awareness;

### Class E — Educational and Capability Communication

Communication intended to build:

* delivery;
* projects;
* incidents;

* actual or suspected incidents;

## 13.8 Stakeholder and Context Analysis

Communication must be designed around the circumstances of the people expected to receive, rely upon, or be affected by it.

The analysis must consider:

* who the stakeholders are;
* what they already know;
* what they reasonably need to know;
* what action is expected;
* what harm could follow misunderstanding;
* language and literacy;
* technical familiarity;
* cultural context;
* disability and accessibility;
* emotional state;
* time pressure;
* age;
* vulnerability;
* economic power;
* dependence on Nexus;
* communication channel;
* privacy expectations;
* ability to question or appeal;
* likely secondary audiences;
* possibility of public redistribution.

The relevant standard is not:

> "Could the author understand this?"

The relevant standard is:

> "Can the intended stakeholder reasonably find, understand, trust, and use the information required for informed action?"

## 13.9 Communication Authority

Communication authority must be explicit, bounded, current, and traceable.

Authority must identify:

* authorised person, role, agent, or unit;
* communication class;
* subject domain;
* intended audience;
* permitted channels;
* geographical or legal jurisdiction;
* allowed claim classes;
* financial or contractual limits;
* approval requirements;
* escalation triggers;
* commencement date;
* expiry or review date;
* revocation authority.

Possession of a password, publishing credential, social account, mailing list, model, communications platform, brand asset, or stakeholder contact does not create communication authority.

### 13.9.1 Representation Rule

A person or agent must not imply that:

* a personal opinion is an organisational position;
* a draft decision is approved;
* preparatory work is a public commitment;
* a forecast is a guarantee;
* an experiment is a production capability;
* a partner speaks for Nexus without authority;
* an agent independently owns a constitutional position;
* silence constitutes organisational approval.

### 13.9.2 Spokesperson Authority

Spokesperson authority must be established for:

* constitutional matters;
* governance matters;
* legal and regulatory matters;
* technical matters;
* incidents and crises;
* commercial claims;
* stakeholder complaints;
* media engagement;
* product announcements;
* public commitments.

The authorised spokesperson may rely on subject-matter experts but remains responsible for ensuring that the communication stays within approved authority.

### 13.9.3 Revocation

Communication authority must be suspended or revoked where:

* the authority has expired;
* the person or agent changes role;
* evidence is no longer current;
* a material incident is underway;
* repeated inaccuracies occur;
* a conflict of interest emerges;
* credentials are compromised;
* brand or constitutional drift is detected;
* required supervision is unavailable.

## 13.10 Constitutional Brand Inheritance

Every Nexus brand, product, service, initiative, project, subsidiary, campaign, partner expression, and agent identity must inherit constitutional obligations.

Brand inheritance flows in the following order:

1. Founder's Philosophy;
2. Founding Charter;
3. Constitutional principles;
4. Constitutional Operations;
5. applicable framework chapters;
6. organisational identity;
7. approved brand architecture;
8. product, service, unit, or campaign expression;
9. channel-specific adaptation.

A lower layer may specialise or adapt the expression.

It may not contradict a higher layer.

### 13.10.1 Required Inheritance

Every authorised brand expression must preserve:

* truthful representation;
* human capability expansion;
* empowerment over dependency;
* evidence integrity;
* accessibility;
* stakeholder dignity;
* lawful conduct;
* authority boundaries;
* promise–experience parity;
* correction obligations;
* protection against manipulation.

### 13.10.2 Brand Charter

Each material brand must maintain a Brand Charter defining:

* purpose;
* relationship to Nexus;
* stakeholders;
* value proposition;
* approved claims;
* prohibited claims;
* identity elements;
* voice and tone;
* accessibility rules;
* evidence requirements;
* legal qualifiers;
* representation authority;
* relationship to other brands;

### 13.10.3 Brand Independence

A brand may possess a distinct:

* name;
* tone;
* visual identity;
* audience;
* market position;
* product promise;
* communication style.

It may not possess an independent constitutional truth.

### 13.10.4 Constitutional Brand Drift Indicators

Potential drift includes:

* promises exceeding delivery capability;
* inflated or unverified success claims;
* inconsistent stakeholder treatment;
* increased reliance on fear, urgency, or dependency;
* reduced disclosure;
* inaccessible communication;
* unapproved identity changes;
* agents speaking beyond authority;
* promotional use of constitutional language unsupported by conduct;
* customer experience that repeatedly contradicts advertised values;
* public claims remaining active after supporting evidence expires.

Material drift requires review, correction, suspension, or withdrawal.

## 13.11 Public Claim Governance

Every material claim must be:

* identifiable;
* classified;
* owned;
* evidenced;
* appropriately qualified;
* approved;
* dated;
* monitored;
* withdrawn when no longer valid.

### 13.11.1 Claim Classes

Claims include:

1. **Verified fact** — directly supported by reliable evidence.
2. **Interpretation** — a reasoned conclusion drawn from stated evidence.
3. **Estimate** — a calculated approximation with assumptions and range.
4. **Forecast** — a future-oriented assessment containing uncertainty.
5. **Aspiration** — an intended future state not yet achieved.
6. **Commitment** — an authorised promise with an accountable owner.
7. **Comparison** — a representation relative to another product, service, method, or baseline.
8. **Performance claim** — a statement about capability, speed, quality, outcomes, reliability, or efficiency.
9. **Quantitative claim** — a claim expressed using numbers, percentages, rankings, savings, time, volume, or probability.
10. **Testimonial or endorsement** — another person's represented experience or opinion.
11. **Safety, health, legal, financial, or technical claim** — a higher-risk specialised representation.
12. **Artificial-intelligence claim** — a statement about AI capability, autonomy, accuracy, reasoning, learning, safety, or performance.
13. **Environmental or social claim** — a statement about impact, sustainability, ethics, inclusion, or community value.

### 13.11.2 Claim Evidence Threshold

Evidence requirements increase with:

* claim specificity;
* stakeholder vulnerability;
* financial influence;
* safety consequence;
* legal consequence;
* novelty;
* scale of distribution;
* difficulty of independent verification;
* expected duration;
* irreversibility of stakeholder action;
* reputational consequence.

Claims must not be made merely because contrary evidence has not been found.

Absence of disproof is not proof.

### 13.11.3 Claim Passport

Every material reusable claim must have a Claim Passport containing:

* unique identifier;
* exact approved wording;
* intended meaning;
* claim class;
* evidence;
* evidence owner;
* claim owner;
* assumptions;
* conditions;
* exclusions;
* authorised audiences;
* authorised channels;
* jurisdictions;
* approval;
* approval date;
* expiry or review date;
* current status;
* known challenges;
* correction history.

### 13.11.4 Overall Impression

Claim validity must be assessed according to the overall impression created by:

* words;
* images;
* sequence;
* emphasis;
* omissions;
* qualifications;
* audio;
* demonstrations;
* design;
* placement;
* timing;
* surrounding context.

A small disclaimer cannot constitutionally repair a dominant misleading impression.

### 13.11.5 Evidence Expiry

A claim must be reviewed when:

* the supporting source expires;
* the product changes;
* operating conditions change;
* pricing changes;
* the sample becomes unrepresentative;
* new contradictory evidence appears;
* a regulator or recognised authority changes its position;
* the claim is applied to a new audience or jurisdiction;
* a material complaint is received.

## 13.12 Promise–Experience Parity

Before creating a public promise, Nexus must determine whether the organisation can support it through actual delivery.

The parity assessment must test:

* current capability;
* available resources;
* authorised delivery process;
* quality controls;
* accessibility;
* dependencies;
* operating constraints;
* geographic limits;
* capacity limits;
* service conditions;
* known failure modes;
* customer support;
* recovery arrangements;
* measurable outcomes;
* evidence from real operation.

A future capability may be described only as:

* proposed;
* planned;
* under development;
* being tested;
* limited;
* experimental;
* subject to approval;

when those descriptions are accurate.

A prototype must not be represented as a production service.

A planned integration must not be represented as operating.

A pilot result must not be generalised without sufficient evidence.

A prepared action must not be presented as authorised execution.

A technical capability must not be presented as an organisational commitment.

### 13.12.1 Public Release Dependency

Where a communication creates material expectations about a product, service, feature, policy, deadline, price, outcome, or support level, its release must be coordinated with the Chapter 12 release process.

Communication readiness does not establish delivery readiness.

## 13.13 Marketing and Commercial Communication

Marketing must assist stakeholders to make informed choices.

It must not be designed primarily to bypass deliberation.

Marketing and advertising must be clearly distinguishable from independent journalism, research, customer opinion, education, community content, or impartial advice.

### 13.13.1 Prohibited Commercial Practices

Nexus must not knowingly use:

* fabricated scarcity;
* false countdowns;
* hidden recurring charges;
* misleading price comparisons;
* incomplete total pricing;
* fabricated reviews;
* undisclosed paid endorsements;
* disguised advertising;
* inaccessible material conditions;
* preselected consent intended to defeat informed choice;
* misleading demonstrations;
* manipulated before-and-after comparisons;
* exaggerated performance;
* fabricated local presence;
* fabricated partnerships;
* unverified professional status;
* coercive cancellation processes;
* fear-based claims unsupported by evidence;
* pressure directed at vulnerable stakeholders;
* synthetic testimonials represented as genuine;
* competitor comparisons without an appropriate basis;
* design patterns intended to conceal refusal, withdrawal, or cancellation.

### 13.13.2 Pricing Communication

Price communication must disclose, where applicable:

* total payable amount;
* taxes;
* recurring charges;
* minimum terms;
* renewal conditions;
* cancellation conditions;
* material exclusions;
* usage limits;
* variable components;
* expected additional costs;
* conditions attached to discounts;
* expiry of offers.

### 13.13.3 Testimonials, Endorsements and Reviews

Testimonials and endorsements must:

* come from real, identifiable experiences unless anonymity is justified;
* remain faithful to the stakeholder's meaning;
* disclose material relationships;
* avoid implying typicality where the experience is exceptional;
* have documented consent;
* be withdrawn when consent or validity ends;
* not be selectively edited into a materially different claim.

Reviews must not be:

* purchased without disclosure;
* generated synthetically and represented as genuine;
* suppressed solely because they are negative;
* altered without author permission;
* attributed to non-existent stakeholders.

### 13.13.4 Educational Marketing

Educational content may support commercial objectives, but it must not:

* disguise its commercial purpose where material;
* withhold essential knowledge solely to manufacture dependency;
* overstate danger to create demand;
* present sales opinion as independent technical doctrine;
* make professional, regulatory, safety, or standards claims without appropriate evidence.

## 13.14 Use of the Founder's Philosophy

The Founder's Philosophy may be communicated publicly only where its use remains faithful to its constitutional meaning.

It must not be reduced to:

* a slogan detached from conduct;
* a decorative statement;
* founder worship;
* an unchallengeable personal opinion;
* a justification for unlimited authority;
* a claim of moral superiority;
* a substitute for evidence;
* a promise that Nexus can realise every person's potential;

When used publicly, it should connect to observable commitments such as:

* accessible knowledge;
* practical learning;
* informed action;
* removal of avoidable barriers;
* respectful stakeholder treatment;
* measurable capability development.

The philosophy must remain attributable to the constitutional foundation while being capable of surviving leadership succession.

## 13.15 Public Commitments

No material public commitment may be issued without:

* appropriate authority;
* a defined commitment owner;
* an identified beneficiary or stakeholder group;
* a clear scope;
* a delivery mechanism;
* allocated resources;
* dependencies;
* conditions;
* risks;
* measurement;
* review date;
* escalation process;
* correction or withdrawal process.

Public commitments must be entered into the Public Commitment Register.

### 13.15.1 Commitment Status

Each commitment must be classified as:

* proposed;
* pending approval;
* approved;
* active;
* at risk;
* partially fulfilled;
* fulfilled;
* superseded;
* withdrawn;
* breached.

### 13.15.2 Commitment Breach

Where a material commitment may not be fulfilled, Nexus must:

1. identify the affected stakeholders;
2. establish the facts;
3. notify the accountable authority;
4. prevent additional unsupported promotion;
5. communicate promptly where stakeholder action is required;
6. explain the effect without concealing material facts;
7. provide remedy where appropriate;
8. update the commitment record;
9. investigate the cause;
10. prevent recurrence.

Silence must not be used to allow a known failed commitment to remain publicly active.

## 13.16 Artificial-Intelligence-Generated Communication

Artificial intelligence may assist with:

* research;
* drafting;
* translation;
* summarisation;
* adaptation;
* personalisation;
* accessibility;
* analysis;
* monitoring;
* distribution preparation.

Artificial intelligence must not independently establish:

* constitutional position;
* communication authority;
* consent;
* material evidence validity;
* public commitment;
* legal interpretation;
* crisis position;
* final approval for high-risk communication.

### 13.16.1 Human Accountability

Every material AI-assisted communication must remain attributable to an accountable human role or authorised governance body.

The responsible person must not approve content merely because:

* it is fluent;
* it appears confident;
* it contains citations;
* it resembles previous approved content;
* a model labels it accurate;
* another agent approved it;
* it was generated from a large body of data.

### 13.16.2 Required Controls

Controls must be proportionate to risk and may include:

* source verification;
* claim extraction;
* citation validation;
* privacy screening;
* confidential-information screening;
* bias and accessibility review;
* hallucination testing;
* human approval;
* version preservation;
* prompt and model provenance;
* synthetic-media labelling;
* impersonation controls;
* expiry and revalidation.

### 13.16.3 Prohibited AI Communication Practices

AI must not be used to:

* fabricate evidence;
* fabricate citations;
* fabricate customers or testimonials;
* impersonate a real person without authority and consent;
* create a false impression of human interaction where that distinction is material;
* generate deceptive reviews;
* conceal sponsorship;
* manipulate vulnerable stakeholders;
* publish confidential information;
* infer sensitive personal characteristics for persuasive targeting without lawful authority;
* create synthetic crisis evidence;
* manufacture false public consensus;
* mass-distribute unverified claims;
* conceal the accountable human authority.

### 13.16.4 AI Disclosure

AI involvement must be disclosed where its absence would create a materially false impression regarding:

* authorship;
* human review;
* expertise;
* personal interaction;
* evidence;
* authenticity;
* identity;
* independence;
* media reality;
* decision authority.

Disclosure must be understandable and appropriately positioned.

A generic statement hidden in terms and conditions is insufficient where the communication itself creates a materially different impression.

### 13.16.5 AI Provenance

Material AI-generated communications must preserve, where proportionate:

* system or model used;
* date;
* purpose;
* human requester;
* source materials;
* instructions;
* material transformations;
* reviewers;
* approval;
* published version;
* later corrections.

## 13.17 Transparency, Qualification and Uncertainty

Communication must distinguish clearly between:

* known fact;
* supported conclusion;
* assumption;
* estimate;
* forecast;
* opinion;
* proposal;
* aspiration;
* approved commitment;
* unresolved question.

Material uncertainty must not be concealed through confident language.

Qualifiers must be:

* visible;
* understandable;
* proximate to the claim;
* proportionate;
* consistent with the main message.

Nexus must communicate dates and versions where information may become stale.

Where information is preliminary, the communication must identify:

* what is known;
* what remains unknown;
* what is being investigated;
* what action is being taken;
* when further information is expected, where that expectation is authorised;
* what stakeholders should do now.

## 13.18 Corrections, Retractions and Record Repair

Nexus must maintain a correction process capable of restoring the information environment after material error.

### 13.18.1 Correction Levels

#### Level 1 — Minor Clarification

Used where the original communication remains materially correct but could be clearer.

#### Level 2 — Material Correction

Used where an error may affect stakeholder understanding, decisions, obligations, cost, safety, or trust.

#### Level 3 — Retraction

Used where the central claim or communication is materially unreliable, unauthorised, deceptive, unsafe, or unsupported.

#### Level 4 — Constitutional or Public Trust Incident

Used where communication reflects systemic misconduct, deliberate manipulation, significant public harm, constitutional drift, or repeated governance failure.

### 13.18.2 Correction Requirements

A correction must:

* identify the affected communication;
* state what was wrong;
* provide the corrected information;
* explain the practical effect where material;
* reach a reasonably equivalent audience;
* avoid burying the correction;
* update controlled copies;
* preserve the historical record;
* identify the accountable owner;
* record corrective and preventive action.

Deleting a communication without explanation may be insufficient where stakeholders could continue relying on it.

### 13.18.3 Non-Retaliation

Employees, contractors, partners, stakeholders, and agents must be able to flag possible communication errors without retaliation for good-faith reporting.

## 13.19 Crisis and Incident Communication

Crisis communication must balance:

* speed;
* accuracy;
* safety;
* privacy;
* legal obligations;
* operational continuity;
* stakeholder needs;
* investigation integrity;
* public trust.

### 13.19.1 Crisis Communication Principles

During a crisis, Nexus must:

1. establish an authorised crisis communication lead;
2. create a verified source of truth;
3. separate confirmed facts from assumptions;
4. identify affected stakeholders;
5. prioritise safety-critical communication;
6. acknowledge material uncertainty;
7. coordinate operational, legal, security, privacy, and leadership functions;
8. avoid speculation;
9. communicate required stakeholder actions clearly;
10. provide updates at a cadence appropriate to the event;
11. correct errors rapidly;
12. preserve decisions and messages;
13. review the communication response after the incident.

### 13.19.2 First Communication

The absence of complete information does not always justify silence.

An initial communication may appropriately state:

* that Nexus is aware of the matter;
* what has been confirmed;
* what has not been confirmed;
* what immediate protective action has occurred;
* what affected stakeholders should do;
* where authoritative updates will appear.

It must not imply that the event is resolved merely because investigation has commenced.

### 13.19.3 Single Source of Truth

Material crisis communications must be coordinated through an identified authoritative source.

Local adaptation is permitted only where:

* facts remain consistent;
* authority is preserved;
* stakeholder-specific needs justify the adaptation;
* changes are traceable.

### 13.19.4 Post-Crisis Review

The review must evaluate:

* accuracy;
* speed;
* accessibility;
* stakeholder reach;
* conflicting messages;
* approval delays;
* misinformation;
* privacy;
* spokesperson performance;
* remedy;
* trust effects;
* required doctrine changes.

## 13.20 Misinformation, Disinformation and Manipulation

Nexus must maintain proportionate capability to identify and respond to materially false information affecting:

* stakeholder safety;
* organisational identity;
* products and services;
* public commitments;
* constitutional legitimacy;
* employees and partners;
* material decisions;
* public trust.

### 13.20.1 Response Assessment

Not every inaccurate statement requires public response.

The assessment must consider:

* source;
* reach;
* credibility;
* velocity;
* affected stakeholders;
* potential harm;
* likelihood of amplification;
* available evidence;
* privacy;
* legal implications;
* whether correction may increase exposure.

### 13.20.2 Permitted Responses

Responses may include:

* monitoring;
* private clarification;
* public correction;
* evidence publication;
* stakeholder notification;
* platform reporting;
* legal escalation;
* security response;
* temporary communication suspension;
* authoritative frequently asked questions;
* pre-emptive education.

### 13.20.3 Constitutional Restraint

Nexus must not respond to misinformation by:

* creating counter-misinformation;
* fabricating consensus;
* harassing critics;
* suppressing legitimate dissent;
* misrepresenting criticism;
* deploying deceptive identities;
* publishing personal information as retaliation;
* manipulating search or discussion through false participation.

Criticism is not misinformation merely because it is unfavourable.

## 13.21 Media and Stakeholder Representation

Media and public stakeholder engagement must be prepared according to:

* subject authority;
* evidence;
* likely questions;
* confidential boundaries;
* unresolved issues;
* public commitments;
* legal and regulatory considerations;
* stakeholder impact;
* correction procedures.

Authorised representatives must distinguish:

* personal opinion;
* technical judgement;
* organisational position;
* constitutional position;
* preliminary information;
* approved commitment.

"Off the record," "background," or similar arrangements must not be assumed.

Any such arrangement requires explicit mutual understanding and must remain within authority and confidentiality obligations.

## 13.22 Internal Communication

Internal communication must support coordinated action and institutional memory.

Material internal communication must make clear:

* what has been decided;
* who decided it;
* the authority used;
* why it was decided;
* what evidence supports it;
* what remains uncertain;
* who owns each action;
* required dates;
* dependencies;
* escalation points;
* what must be preserved.

### 13.22.1 Internal Truthfulness

Nexus must not maintain a truthful external appearance through an internally misleading information environment.

Internal communications must not conceal:

* known delivery failures;
* material risks;
* customer harm;
* regulatory issues;
* security or privacy incidents;
* financial exposure;
* evidence contradiction;
* constitutional drift.

### 13.22.2 Dissent and Challenge

Internal communication systems must allow:

* questions;
* dissent;
* correction;
* escalation;
* minority reports;
* evidence challenges;
* declaration of uncertainty.

Disagreement must not be compressed into false consensus.

### 13.22.3 Decision Communication

Decisions must be communicated to affected parties with enough context to permit correct execution.

A decision that cannot be understood, located, attributed, or operationalised has not been communicated adequately.

## 13.23 Accessibility and Comprehension

Communication must be usable by the intended stakeholder population.

Accessibility must consider:

* visual access;
* auditory access;
* motor access;
* cognitive access;
* language;
* literacy;
* technical competence;
* device limitations;
* connectivity;
* environmental conditions;
* time available;
* emotional load.

### 13.23.1 Plain Language

Public and operational communications should use the clearest language compatible with accuracy.

Plain language does not require removing necessary technical detail.

It requires:

* logical organisation;
* understandable wording;
* usable structure;
* information that readers can find;
* information that readers can understand;
* information that readers can apply.

### 13.23.2 Layered Communication

Where audiences possess different knowledge levels, communication should use layers such as:

1. essential action;
2. concise explanation;
3. detailed reasoning;
4. evidence and technical material;
5. formal or legal source.

### 13.23.3 Alternative Formats

Material communication may require:

* text alternatives;
* captions;
* transcripts;
* audio;
* accessible documents;
* keyboard-operable interfaces;
* mobile-compatible layouts;
* translated versions;
* simplified summaries;
* diagrams with explanations;
* human assistance.

### 13.23.4 Accessibility Assurance

Accessibility must be considered during design and tested before release.

It must not be treated solely as a later correction or compliance statement.

## 13.24 Privacy, Consent and Human Dignity

Communication must protect personal information and human dignity.

Nexus must not publish identifiable personal information, experiences, images, recordings, case studies, testimonials, or stories without appropriate authority and consent.

Consent must be:

* informed;
* specific enough for the intended use;
* recorded;
* revocable where applicable;
* separated from coercive service conditions;
* reviewed when the use materially changes.

Particular care is required where communication involves:

* children;
* vulnerable persons;
* health;
* trauma;
* financial distress;
* employment;
* complaints;
* incidents;
* cultural identity;
* disability;
* personal loss;
* safety events.

Consent does not remove the obligation to prevent exploitation or foreseeable harm.

## 13.27 Public Trust

Public trust must be treated as a consequence of repeated trustworthy conduct.

It must not be treated merely as:

* awareness;
* popularity;
* sentiment;
* reach;
* engagement;
* ratings;
* public relations success.

Trustworthiness requires:

* competence;
* honesty;
* consistency;
* accessibility;
* accountability;
* appropriate transparency;
* fair treatment;
* fulfilment of commitments;
* willingness to correct error.

### 13.27.1 Trust Measures

Measures may include:

* claim correction rate;
* commitment fulfilment;
* complaint resolution;
* stakeholder comprehension;
* accessibility performance;
* evidence coverage;
* public response accuracy;
* correction speed;
* repeated issue rate;
* promise–experience variance;
* stakeholder willingness to rely on information;
* trust recovery after failure.

### 13.27.2 Anti-Gaming

Trust measures must not be improved artificially by:

* suppressing complaints;
* removing criticism;
* selecting only favourable respondents;
* purchasing reviews;
* changing definitions;
* excluding affected stakeholders;
* measuring awareness as trust;
* rewarding communication volume;
* delaying recognition of failed commitments.

## 13.31 Communication Gates

### Gate 1 — Purpose Gate

Does the communication serve a legitimate constitutional, stakeholder, operational, educational, or commercial purpose?

### Gate 2 — Authority Gate

Is the communicator authorised for this subject, audience, channel, and claim?

### Gate 3 — Evidence Gate

Are material claims supportable, current, and traceable?

### Gate 4 — Experience Gate

Can operational reality support the expectation created?

### Gate 5 — Stakeholder Gate

Can the intended audience reasonably understand and act on the communication?

### Gate 6 — Risk Gate

Have safety, privacy, legal, security, reputational, accessibility, and vulnerability risks been assessed?

### Gate 7 — Identity Gate

Does the communication express constitutional identity without distortion?

### Gate 8 — Approval Gate

Have all required accountable authorities approved the exact release version?

### Gate 9 — Record Gate

Can the communication, evidence, authority, version, and correction history be reconstructed?

Failure of a mandatory gate blocks publication.

## 13.32 Mandatory Stop and Escalation Conditions

Communication must be stopped or escalated where:

* evidence cannot be verified;
* authority is unclear;
* a material legal or regulatory issue exists;
* the communication could expose confidential information;
* consent is absent;
* operational capability cannot support the promise;
* a vulnerable stakeholder may be materially harmed;
* the communication creates a misleading overall impression;
* an AI system has fabricated sources or identities;
* a public commitment lacks an owner or resources;
* crisis facts are disputed;
* a material correction is unresolved;
* constitutional identity is being used to justify conduct contrary to the Charter;
* publication would interfere with safety, security, investigation, or remedy.

Urgency may increase escalation priority.

It does not remove the stop condition.

## 13.38 Evidence and Standards Anchors

This framework is a Nexus constitutional instrument rather than a claim of external certification.

Its design is informed by recognised governance, advertising, accessibility, plain-language, privacy, and crisis-management principles.

ISO 37000 places organisational purpose, values, stakeholder value, accountability, and responsible governance at the centre of organisational governance.

Australian Competition and Consumer Commission guidance states that advertised claims should be true, accurate, based on reasonable grounds, and capable of proof. The same obligations apply to claims made through social media.

The Australian Association of National Advertisers requires advertising to be clearly distinguishable and not disguised as independent content, reviews, research, or user-generated material.

ISO 24495-1 establishes governing principles for creating plain-language documents, while WCAG 2.2 provides testable accessibility requirements organised around perceivable, operable, understandable, and robust content.

The Office of the Australian Information Commissioner states that privacy obligations apply when commercially available AI products handle personal information, including content-generation and productivity systems.

ISO crisis and incident-management guidance emphasises defined responsibilities, coordinated action, shared direction, and structured incident management.

These sources inform implementation and later validation but do not replace jurisdiction-specific legal, privacy, accessibility, advertising, professional, or regulatory review.

## 13.39 Operational Doctrine

> Communicate only what Nexus is authorised and able to support.
> Make material claims traceable to current evidence.
> Distinguish fact, interpretation, forecast, aspiration, and commitment.
> Design communication for stakeholder understanding and informed action.
> Do not disguise advertising, automation, uncertainty, sponsorship, or material limitations.
> Protect privacy, dignity, accessibility, and vulnerable stakeholders.
> Keep public promises owned, resourced, monitored, and correctable.
> Artificial intelligence may assist communication but never removes human accountability.
> Correct material error visibly and proportionately.
> Treat public trust as the consequence of trustworthy behaviour.
> Brand must express constitutional identity rather than conceal operational reality.
