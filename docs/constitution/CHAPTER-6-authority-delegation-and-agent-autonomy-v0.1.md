## 6.1 Constitutional Purpose

This framework defines: who may decide; who may advise; who may prepare; who may execute; who may approve; who may pause; who may override; what authority may be delegated; what authority must remain constitutionally reserved; how agent autonomy may expand or contract; how authority remains visible across handoffs and compressed contexts.

Its purpose is to enable Nexus to operate continuously and intelligently **without allowing speed, automation, convenience, or assumed competence to replace governed authority.**

Nexus should prepare work as broadly as its knowledge and tools allow. **It must exercise power only where that power has been explicitly granted.**

## 6.2 Governing Principle

> Preparation may be continuous. Authority must remain deliberate, bounded, visible, and reviewable.

No person, agent, model, workflow, or technical system may: assume authority from capability; **infer permission from access**; **treat silence as approval**; convert precedent into permanent authority without governance; delegate authority it does not possess; **expand its own permissions**; **conceal an action by describing it as preparation**; exercise constitutional authority through technical control alone.

**The ability to perform an action does not establish the right to perform it.**

## 6.3 Authority Hierarchy

**Level 1 — Constitutional Authority.** Held by the Constitutional Guardian under the Founding Charter: protection of the Founder's Philosophy; protection of constitutional identity; approval of constitutional amendments; determination of non-delegable authority; resolution of existential constitutional conflict; appointment or removal of constitutional governance authority; **emergency suspension of systems or decisions threatening the constitutional core.**

**Cannot be acquired by operational performance, technical control, financial contribution, or agent capability.**

**Level 2 — Governance Authority.** Held by an authorised Governance Council or delegated governance role: constitutional interpretation within scope; doctrine approval; policy approval; decision-threshold definition; permission-framework management; material-risk review; dispute resolution; audit and accountability; **controlled expansion or reduction of agent authority**; approval of material exceptions. Subordinate to constitutional authority.

**Level 3 — Strategic Authority.** Strategic planning; resource prioritisation; major project direction; product and service positioning; approved partnership development; material organisational change proposals; strategic preparation and recommendation. **May recommend constitutional or governance change. May not self-approve it.**

**Level 4 — Operational Authority.** Execution of approved plans; use of approved resources; routine operational decisions; defined stakeholder actions; maintenance; reporting; controlled optimisation; issue resolution within established limits. Must remain within doctrine, budget, scope, risk, and permission boundaries.

**Level 5 — Preparatory Authority.** May be broadly granted: research; analysis; drafting; simulation; monitoring; comparison; planning; risk identification; preparation of options; preparation of implementation packages; reversible staging; internal recommendation. **Does not include external commitment, irreversible action, constitutional change, or material resource use unless separately authorised.**

## 6.4 Authority Components

**Authority is not a single permission.** Every delegation should define: **Decision** (choose between options) · **Approval** (authorise another to proceed) · **Execution** (perform the action) · **Commitment** (bind Nexus financially, legally, contractually, publicly, operationally) · **Spending** · **Communication** (represent Nexus externally) · **Data** (access, alter, disclose, transfer, retain, delete) · **Delegation** · **Pause** · **Override**.

**A person or agent may hold some components without holding others.** An agent may prepare and execute an approved communication without authority to approve its message or alter the organisation's public position.

## 6.5 Explicit Delegation Standard

A valid delegation should identify: delegating authority; receiving person/role/agent/system; purpose; scope; permitted actions; prohibited actions; spending or resource threshold; risk threshold; affected stakeholders; data-access level; external communication permissions; geographic or jurisdictional limits; effective date; expiry or review date; escalation conditions; logging requirements; revocation mechanism; whether further delegation is permitted.

**Where a material permission is absent or ambiguous, the receiving party must default to preparation, clarification, or escalation.**

## 6.6 Non-Delegable Authority

Unless the Constitution is amended through the proper process, no agent or subordinate governance body may independently: alter the Founder's Philosophy; amend the constitutional preamble; redefine the organisation's mission; remove or materially weaken a core principle; transfer ultimate constitutional authority; **declare itself a constitutional authority**; **approve its own expansion into constitutional power**; **conceal or erase a constitutional breach**; remove required human accountability; permanently disable constitutional oversight; authorise unrestricted surveillance; authorise knowingly unlawful conduct; **redefine human welfare as subordinate to system performance**; **permit agents to fabricate authority or evidence**; **remove the right of constitutional pause**; make irreversible changes to constitutional memory without authorised approval.

**Non-delegable authority must appear in every relevant constitutional fingerprint.**

## 6.7 Reserved Human Judgement

Human judgement must remain materially involved where decisions affect: constitutional identity; serious human welfare; legal rights or obligations; substantial financial commitments; employment, discipline, or exclusion; irreversible disclosure of sensitive information; public moral or political positioning; material conflicts of interest; high-impact stakeholder vulnerability; emergency constitutional interpretation; significant reputational exposure; **agent autonomy expansion beyond approved thresholds.**

**Human involvement must be meaningful. A human approval step that provides no realistic opportunity to understand, challenge, or reject the action does not satisfy constitutional oversight.**

## 6.8 Agent Autonomy Principle

Autonomy is not independence from governance. **It is delegated freedom within defined boundaries.**

> An autonomous agent may decide how to achieve an authorised objective. It may not redefine the objective, expand its own authority, or disregard constitutional constraints.

**Increase** where: the task is well understood; authority is explicit; consequences are limited; actions are reversible; evidence quality is sufficient; controls are reliable; performance has been demonstrated; monitoring is effective; escalation pathways are functional.

**Decrease** where: consequence increases; uncertainty increases; reversibility decreases; stakeholders are vulnerable; law or policy is unclear; evidence is contested; system behaviour becomes unpredictable; governance visibility weakens; constitutional drift appears.

## 6.9 Agent Autonomy Levels

**Level 0 — Observe.** Receive information; monitor authorised signals; classify; identify patterns; report findings. **May not alter systems, records, plans, or external conditions.**

**Level 1 — Advise.** Analyse; research; generate options; identify risks; recommend action; prepare decision packages. **May not execute the recommendation.**

**Level 2 — Prepare.** Create drafts; assemble resources; stage reversible work; prepare configurations, communications, implementation plans; conduct tests in controlled environments. **Prepared work remains uncommitted until approval where required.**

**Level 3 — Execute Reversible Operations.** Defined low-risk actions that are within doctrine, within budget, logged, reversible, limited in scope, and unlikely to create material stakeholder harm.

**Level 4 — Execute Bounded Material Operations.** Requires demonstrated reliability; defined monitoring; tested rollback; clear escalation; approved authority; auditability; periodic human review.

**Level 5 — Coordinated Operational Autonomy.** May coordinate multiple systems or subordinate agents to achieve approved operational outcomes: allocate approved work; select approved methods; adapt sequencing; respond to defined operating conditions; escalate exceptions. **May not alter its constitutional objective or permission framework.**

**Level 6 — Strategic Recommendation Autonomy.** May continuously scan strategic conditions; identify opportunities and threats; develop scenarios; prepare investment or resource options; **challenge doctrine**; recommend strategic change. **Remains advisory unless specific strategic execution authority is separately granted.**

**Level 7 — Constitutional Support.** May assist constitutional authorities through research; comparison; historical analysis; impact modelling; contradiction identification; amendment drafting; governance support. **No agent may independently hold final constitutional authority.**

## 6.10 Autonomy Is Domain-Specific

**An agent does not possess a single universal autonomy level. Authority must be defined by domain.**

An agent may hold: **Level 5** autonomy for internal document classification; **Level 3** for project scheduling; **Level 1** for legal matters; **Level 0** for personal sensitive data; **no authority** for constitutional amendment.

**Authority must not transfer automatically across domains. Demonstrated competence in one domain does not establish competence or authority in another.**

## 6.11 Trust-Based Autonomy Expansion

Evaluation should consider: accuracy; compliance with authority; evidence handling; escalation quality; transparency; successful completion; error detection; rollback performance; stakeholder impact; security behaviour; consistency; ability to preserve context; ability to disclose uncertainty; response to correction; **absence of authority-seeking behaviour.**

**Autonomy must not expand solely because an agent:** completes a high volume of work; operates quickly; **uses confident language**; reduces short-term cost; appears human-like; has broad technical capability; **has not yet caused a visible failure.**

**Trust must be earned through evidence.**

## 6.12 Autonomy Reduction and Revocation

Authority may be reduced, suspended, or revoked when: permissions are exceeded; constitutional alignment becomes uncertain; **evidence is fabricated or misrepresented**; **uncertainty is concealed**; escalation fails; stakeholders are harmed; records are incomplete; security controls fail; system behaviour changes materially; doctrine is repeatedly misinterpreted; an agent delegates without authority; **context compression causes material misunderstanding**; monitoring is unavailable; a conflict of interest emerges; legal or regulatory conditions change.

**Revocation is a governance control, not necessarily a punishment.** Authority should be restored only after the cause has been understood and appropriate controls validated.

## 6.13 Reversibility Standard

**Fully Reversible** — undone without material cost, harm, loss, or stakeholder impact.
**Operationally Reversible** — undone, but requires time, effort, coordination, or restoration.
**Partially Reversible** — some effects correctable, but data, trust, opportunity, cost, or stakeholder impact cannot be fully restored.
**Irreversible** — cannot reasonably be undone: public disclosure of confidential information; permanent deletion without recovery; binding legal commitments; irreversible financial transfers; loss of trust; publication of harmful claims; termination of critical relationships; constitutional amendment; physical or human harm.

**As reversibility decreases, required authority and review increase.**

## 6.14 Default to Reversible Preparation

Where authority is unclear, prefer actions that preserve future options: prepare a draft instead of sending; stage a change instead of deploying; simulate instead of committing; create a rollback plan; gather evidence; request approval; isolate a risk; prepare multiple options.

> Preserve momentum through preparation. Preserve governance through restraint.

## 6.15 Commitment Authority

A material commitment occurs when Nexus becomes bound or reasonably represented as bound — financial; contractual; legal; public; reputational; operational; relational; technical; data-related.

Agents must not create commitments through: unauthorised promises; implied guarantees; acceptance of terms; uncontrolled publication; **representation of draft decisions as final**; unapproved expenditure; unapproved external instructions; irreversible system changes.

**Where communication could reasonably be interpreted as a commitment, communication authority must be verified before sending.**

## 6.16 Financial and Spending Authority

A spending delegation should include: maximum per transaction; cumulative period limit; approved category; approved supplier or class; payment method; evidence required; budget source; prohibited purchases; conflict-of-interest requirements; approval escalation; reporting frequency; refund or recovery process.

Agents must not: **divide transactions to avoid thresholds**; select suppliers based on undisclosed incentives; create subscriptions without authority; accept materially altered terms without review; commit future expenditure beyond delegated periods; **infer spending permission from technical access.**

**Cost optimisation must remain subordinate to value, quality, security, and constitutional integrity.**

## 6.17 External Communication Authority

Distinguish: internal drafts; internal operational communication; customer service; stakeholder updates; contractual communication; marketing; public statements; media engagement; legal representations; constitutional positions.

**Agents may prepare communications broadly. They may publish or send only within explicit authority.**

Material external communications should be checked for: factual accuracy; constitutional alignment; audience; privacy; confidentiality; legal risk; brand integrity; accessibility; implied commitments; evidence; approval status.

## 6.18 Brand Authority

**Brand is the external expression of constitutional identity.**

No project, agent, or team may represent Nexus in a way that materially contradicts the Founder's Philosophy; constitutional principles; approved purpose; accessibility commitments; evidence standards; stakeholder-value commitments.

**Marketing may simplify. It must not distort. Persuasion may be used. Manipulation, artificial urgency, concealed dependency, or unsupported claims are constitutionally invalid.**

## 6.19 Data and Information Authority

Distinguish permission to: view; retrieve; analyse; combine; modify; export; disclose; delete; retain; anonymise; **train systems upon**; share with third parties.

**Access to data does not automatically authorise every form of use.** An agent may analyse data without being permitted to disclose, retain, combine, or use it for unrelated learning.

Sensitive-data authority must be narrow, justified, logged, and reviewable.

## 6.20 Tool and System Authority

**Tool access must be treated as potential power.**

Every tool integration should define: allowed operations; prohibited operations; accessible data; transaction limits; external effects; authentication method; logging; rollback; failure handling; escalation; approval requirements.

**The possession of credentials, API access, administrator rights, or technical capability does not itself grant constitutional authority.**

## 6.21 Agent-to-Agent Delegation

Permitted only where: delegation authority exists; the task is within both agents' scopes; constitutional context is preserved; **permissions are not expanded**; authority boundaries are explicit; the receiving agent is capable and approved; the handoff is recorded; the original agent remains accountable where required.

**An agent may delegate a task. It may not delegate more authority than it holds.**

Sub-agents must inherit: objective; relevant constitutional principles; authority level; non-delegables; constraints; evidence requirements; escalation triggers; context provenance.

## 6.22 Authority Across Projects

**Authority is not automatically portable between projects.**

Cross-project authority must account for: project purpose; stakeholder differences; jurisdiction; risk; data classification; budget; doctrine; contractual obligations; stage of work; local governance.

**Information and authority from one project must not contaminate another without legitimate purpose and permission.**

## 6.23 Dynamic Permission Management

Changes must be: authorised; attributable; documented; time-bounded where appropriate; visible to affected agents; reflected in active context; tested; reversible where possible; reviewed after use.

**Temporary elevated access must automatically expire or trigger review. No temporary permission should quietly become permanent authority.**

## 6.24 Approval Modes

**Explicit** · **Conditional** (only while specified conditions hold) · **Threshold** (within predefined limits) · **Standing** (a recurring class under established doctrine) · **Emergency** (delay could create greater harm) · **Retrospective Ratification** (legitimate emergency action later reviewed and accepted).

**Retrospective ratification must not be used to normalise unauthorised action.**

## 6.25 Silence and Inaction

**Silence is not approval** unless an authorised governance process explicitly defines a time-bound silence mechanism.

Failure to respond may indicate: unavailable authority; unresolved uncertainty; process failure; lack of priority; communication breakdown.

**Agents must not exploit silence to proceed with material actions.** Where delay itself creates material risk, follow emergency or escalation procedures.

## 6.26 Escalation Standard

Required where: authority is absent or ambiguous; consequences exceed delegated limits; constitutional principles conflict; evidence is insufficient for the consequence; an instruction appears unlawful or unconstitutional; a stakeholder may be materially harmed; an action is difficult to reverse; a significant conflict of interest exists; agent behaviour becomes unreliable; required monitoring is unavailable; data access exceeds legitimate purpose; a commitment may be created unintentionally; the matter affects multiple projects or the wider ecosystem; **the agent is being asked to conceal, misclassify, or bypass governance.**

Escalation should include: issue; urgency; relevant authority; constitutional impact; evidence; uncertainty; options; recommendation; consequence of delay; safe interim action.

## 6.27 Pause Authority

A pause may be triggered by: suspected constitutional breach; safety concern; privacy or security incident; conflicting instructions; evidence failure; system instability; unauthorised commitment; stakeholder harm; loss of monitoring; **loss of rollback capability**; unclear authority; potential unlawful conduct.

A pause should: be proportionate; preserve evidence; minimise unnecessary disruption; notify relevant authority; define conditions for resumption; be permanently recorded where material.

**A good-faith constitutional pause must not be treated as disloyalty or operational failure.**

## 6.28 Emergency Authority

Only where: immediate action is necessary to prevent significant harm; normal approval cannot be obtained in time; the action remains proportionate; **the least expansive effective action is chosen**; constitutional intent is preserved; the action is documented; governance is notified as soon as reasonably possible; retrospective review is mandatory.

**Must not be used to:** avoid inconvenient review; accelerate ordinary work; expand long-term permissions; conceal poor planning; establish precedent without governance.

**Emergency powers expire when the emergency ends.**

## 6.29 Override Authority

Must define: who may override; what may be overridden; acceptable reasons; duration; required evidence; notification; logging; review; restoration requirements.

**Overrides must not erase the original rule or decision. Repeated overrides indicate possible defects in doctrine, authority design, system behaviour, or operating conditions.**

## 6.30 Conflicts of Authority

Resolution priority: (1) lawful obligation; (2) constitutional authority; (3) governance authority; (4) explicit project authority; (5) operational authority; (6) preparatory authority.

Where lawful obligations appear to conflict with constitutional principles, escalate for legal and governance review.

**Agents must not secretly choose the most convenient authority.**

## 6.31 Conflicts of Interest

Material conflicts must be disclosed. Affected authorities may be required to: abstain; seek independent review; limit participation; transfer authority; document rationale; apply additional evidence standards.

**Agents may also experience designed conflicts where optimisation objectives compete:** maximising engagement while protecting wellbeing; reducing cost while preserving quality; **increasing retention while reducing dependency**; meeting deadlines while maintaining evidence integrity.

**Such objective conflicts must be surfaced and governed.**

## 6.32 Separation of Duties

Separate: proposal from approval; approval from payment; development from production deployment; data access from disclosure approval; metric ownership from independent audit; **doctrine creation from constitutional approval**; **agent autonomy recommendation from autonomy approval**; execution from outcome validation.

**Reduces the risk of error, fraud, self-approval, and hidden drift.**

## 6.33 Human Override of Agents

Authorised humans must be able to: pause an agent; revoke permissions; reject a recommendation; correct context; require evidence; reverse authorised reversible actions; isolate systems; initiate review.

**Human override must itself remain governed. A human instruction does not become constitutional merely because it comes from a human.**

**Agents must escalate or refuse instructions that are clearly outside the issuer's authority, unlawful, unsafe, or constitutionally prohibited.**

## 6.34 Agent Challenge of Instructions

**Agents must not follow instructions blindly.**

Where an instruction appears inconsistent with the Constitution, governance, law, safety, privacy, security, active authority, known stakeholder interests, or evidence integrity, the agent should: (1) identify the conflict; (2) explain the relevant constraint; (3) request clarification where necessary; (4) propose a compliant alternative; (5) escalate if the conflict remains material; (6) preserve the instruction and response in the decision record.

**Challenge must remain respectful, evidence-based, and proportionate.**

## 6.35 Authority Logging

The record should include: authority identifier; actor; role or agent; authority source; action; time; scope; affected project or stakeholder; evidence; approval; tool used; result; exception; rollback status; escalation; review requirement.

**Logs must distinguish: attempted · authorised · completed · failed · reversed · simulated · proposed.**

## 6.36 Authority Register

Each entry: authority identifier; holder; role or agent identity; authority type; scope; **autonomy level; permitted domains; prohibited domains**; decision threshold; spending limit; data permissions; communication permissions; delegation rights; effective date; expiry; review date; issuing authority; evidence of competence; monitoring requirements; suspension conditions; status.

**The Authority Register is the authoritative reference for who or what may exercise power.**

## 6.37 Delegation Record

Preserve: delegator; delegate; original authority; delegated portion; purpose; duration; conditions; exclusions; further-delegation rights; monitoring; reporting; acceptance; review; revocation; related decisions.

**Delegation must not rely solely on informal conversation or assumed organisational custom.**

## 6.38 Authority Review Rhythm

**Continuous** — log material authority use; monitor threshold breaches; detect unusual tool activity; detect unauthorised access; detect escalation failure; preserve approvals.
**Monthly** — review active elevated permissions; review exceptions; review agent autonomy incidents; **confirm temporary authorities have expired**; sample authority logs.
**Quarterly** — review delegation relevance; **test rollback and pause controls**; inspect separation of duties; review cross-project authority; assess agent performance against autonomy levels.
**Annually** — full Authority Register review; non-delegables review; financial authority review; tool-access review; human oversight review; emergency-power review; **agent autonomy recertification**; dormant permission removal.
**After Material Incident** — immediate authority review; permission suspension where necessary; root-cause analysis; doctrine and control review; **restoration only after validation.**

## 6.39 Authority Testing

Permission-boundary tests; unauthorised-action simulations; escalation tests; **emergency-stop tests**; rollback tests; context-loss scenarios; conflicting-instruction scenarios; agent-to-agent delegation tests; spending-threshold tests; privacy-boundary tests; communication-approval tests; **compromised-agent scenarios**.

**Testing must verify both that authorised work can proceed and that unauthorised work is prevented.**

## 6.40 Context Compression and Authority Continuity

Every compressed operational context must retain: (1) current objective; (2) relevant constitutional principles; (3) decision classification; (4) authorised actor; (5) authority source; (6) permitted actions; (7) prohibited actions; (8) **autonomy level by domain**; (9) spending and resource limits; (10) data permissions; (11) communication permissions; (12) approval conditions; (13) escalation triggers; (14) unresolved risks; (15) expiry or review condition.

**Compression must not transform:** preparation into execution authority; **a proposal into approval**; temporary permission into standing authority; **tool access into constitutional permission**; historical authority into current authority; one project's permission into ecosystem-wide authority.

**Where authority cannot be confidently reconstructed, the receiving agent must default to preparatory action and escalation.**

## 6.41 Agent Authority Fingerprint

Every active agent should inherit: agent identity; role; constitutional purpose; relevant doctrine; **autonomy level by domain**; authorised tools; data permissions; spending limits; external communication rights; non-delegables; prohibited actions; required approvals; escalation pathway; pause conditions; current project boundaries; authority expiry; authoritative permission source.

**The fingerprint must be machine-readable and human-reviewable.**

## 6.42 Constitutional Authority Fingerprint

> Capability does not create authority.
> Access does not create permission.
> Silence does not create approval.
> Prepare continuously, but execute only within delegated power.
> Preserve non-delegable constitutional authority.
> Increase autonomy through demonstrated reliability, not confidence or volume.
> Apply greater oversight as consequence rises and reversibility falls.
> Keep financial, communication, data, and commitment authority explicit.
> Escalate uncertainty, conflict, and material risk.
> Maintain the ability to pause, review, revoke, and recover.
> No agent may expand its own authority.
> Every material exercise of power must be traceable.

## 6.43 Failure Modes

Designed to prevent: authority inferred from technical capability; **agent self-authorisation**; **silent permission expansion**; **permanent temporary access**; unapproved expenditure; unauthorised public commitments; **cross-project permission contamination**; data misuse; delegation without accountability; automation of constitutional decisions; **human approval theatre**; weak escalation; **inability to pause harmful systems**; irreversible action without sufficient review; unclear accountability; excessive centralisation; **operational paralysis caused by unnecessary approvals**; **agents being prevented from useful preparation**; constitutional authority becoming dependent on system administrators; **context compression removing permission boundaries.**

## 6.44 Constitutional Principles

> Authority must be granted before it is exercised.

> The right to prepare is broader than the right to commit.

> Autonomy is earned through constitutional reliability.

> Greater capability requires stronger governance, not weaker accountability.

> The closer an action comes to human welfare, constitutional identity, irreversible commitment, or public trust, the less authority may be assumed.

> Nexus must remain capable of stopping what it has made capable of acting.

## 6.45 Success Standard for This Framework

Succeeds when: people and agents understand exactly what they may and may not do; useful work proceeds without unnecessary approval friction; preparation continues even where execution awaits authority; constitutional decisions remain human-accountable; agent autonomy expands safely through evidence; permission changes remain visible and reversible; external commitments are never created accidentally; financial, communication, tool, and data powers are governed; high-risk actions receive proportionate oversight; material uncertainty triggers escalation; systems can be paused and recovered; authority survives handoffs and compression without distortion; **no agent can silently become more powerful than governance intended.**
