## 5.1 Constitutional Purpose

This framework defines how Nexus creates, receives, evaluates, organises, preserves, retrieves, challenges, updates, and retires knowledge.

Its purpose is to ensure that organisational intelligence remains: grounded in identifiable sources; distinguishable from opinion, assumption, and inference; current enough for its intended use; accessible to authorised people and agents; protected against loss, distortion, duplication, and silent alteration; capable of carrying lessons across projects; challengeable when evidence changes; preserved through agent handoffs and context compression; governed by constitutional purpose rather than accumulated indiscriminately.

**Knowledge is a permanent organisational asset.**

Possession of information alone does not create organisational intelligence. **Intelligence emerges when information is validated, contextualised, connected, applied, reviewed, and made available at the point of decision.**

## 5.2 Governing Principle

> Nexus must know what it knows, know why it believes it, know how reliable it is, and know when it must be questioned again.

No information may be treated as authoritative merely because it: appears in an organisational system; was produced by a senior person; **was generated by artificial intelligence**; has been repeated frequently; supported a previous decision; is convenient; is familiar; **has not yet been challenged.**

Authority arises from constitutional standing, provenance, evidence quality, relevance, and governed adoption.

## 5.3 Constitutional Definition of Organisational Knowledge

Organisational knowledge is information that has been: (1) identified; (2) classified; (3) attributed to a source; (4) assessed for reliability; (5) placed in context; (6) connected to relevant decisions, doctrine, or operations; (7) made retrievable; (8) assigned an owner or custodian; (9) given a review condition; (10) preserved or retired through an authorised process.

Information that does not meet these standards may still be useful, but must be treated as unverified, provisional, contextual, or historical.

## 5.4 Knowledge Hierarchy

**Level 1 — Constitutional Knowledge:** Founder's Philosophy; preamble; constitutional principles; constitutional authority; amendment requirements; constitutional fingerprints; non-delegable boundaries. **May not be silently changed, paraphrased into a materially different meaning, or replaced by operational convenience.**

**Level 2 — Governance Knowledge:** decision rights; approval thresholds; amendment classes; materiality standards; escalation pathways; governance decisions; constitutional interpretations; validation requirements.

**Level 3 — Doctrine:** approved interpretations, standards, practices, and reusable guidance translating constitutional principles into consistent action. Answers: how Nexus normally reasons; how similar situations should be approached; what standards apply across projects; what lessons have been formally adopted; what boundaries agents and teams must inherit. **Authoritative within scope but subordinate to the Constitution.**

**Level 4 — Policy and Procedure:** operational rules, workflows, controls, templates, instructions, defined methods. Must remain traceable to the doctrine or governance requirement they implement.

**Level 5 — Project and Domain Knowledge:** information relevant to a particular project, organisation, customer, product, discipline, jurisdiction, market, technology, or operational environment.

**Level 6 — Working Knowledge:** research notes; drafts; observations; preliminary analysis; meeting notes; temporary assumptions; open questions; unverified sources; active experiments. **Not automatically authoritative.**

**Level 7 — Historical and Retired Knowledge:** superseded, deprecated, rejected, or historically significant information preserved for traceability. Must remain discoverable where it explains past decisions, previous doctrine, failed approaches, amendments, lessons, or organisational evolution. **Must never be presented as current without clear qualification.**

## 5.5 Evidence Classification

**Primary** — direct records or observations: original contracts; legislation; system records; transaction data; direct measurements; first-party statements; original research; recordings; approved constitutional documents.

**Secondary** — interpretation, reporting, analysis, or commentary based on primary evidence.

**Experiential** — derived from direct organisational or stakeholder experience: operational lessons; user feedback; project outcomes; professional judgement; repeated field observations. **Must disclose context and limitations.**

**Inferential** — conclusions derived from patterns, relationships, models, or incomplete observations. **Must be clearly marked and must not be represented as direct fact.**

**Hypothetical** — scenario-based reasoning. May support planning, risk analysis, and testing but **cannot establish that an event has occurred.**

**Anecdotal** — individual examples that may reveal signals but do not establish prevalence. **May initiate investigation. Must not automatically determine doctrine.**

## 5.6 Knowledge Status

**Authoritative** — formally approved and currently governing within scope.
**Validated** — sufficiently supported and reviewed for its intended use but not itself a governing instrument.
**Provisional** — useful now but awaiting confirmation, review, or stronger evidence.
**Contested** — subject to credible disagreement, contradictory evidence, or unresolved interpretation.
**Experimental** — being deliberately tested.
**Historical** — preserved as an accurate record of an earlier state.
**Superseded** — replaced by a newer authorised version.
**Deprecated** — no longer recommended or authorised.
**Rejected** — considered and deliberately not adopted.
**Unknown** — insufficient information to assign a stronger status.

**Status must be visible at the point of use.**

## 5.7 Provenance Standard

Every material knowledge item must include enough provenance to answer: Where did this come from? Who created, observed, or supplied it? When was it created or obtained? Was it altered, summarised, translated, compressed, or interpreted? Who validated or approved it? What original source supports it? What decisions or doctrine rely upon it? What later evidence contradicts or supersedes it? When must it be reviewed?

Provenance must survive: copying; summarisation; export; agent handoff; system migration; format conversion; **context compression.**

**Where exact provenance cannot be preserved, the knowledge must be downgraded or marked as uncertain.**

## 5.8 Source Reliability Assessment

**A source may be highly reliable for one purpose and unsuitable for another.**

Assessment should consider: directness; authority; expertise; independence; methodology; recency; completeness; reproducibility; internal consistency; incentives and conflicts; jurisdiction; relevance; corroboration; known error history.

**High** — direct, authoritative, current, methodologically sound, appropriately scoped.
**Moderate** — credible but with limitations, indirectness, uncertainty, or incomplete validation.
**Low** — unverified, outdated, biased, incomplete, anecdotal, poorly sourced, or methodologically weak.
**Unassessed** — not yet evaluated.

**A high-reliability source does not guarantee that every claim derived from it is correct.** The relationship between the source and the specific claim must still be examined.

## 5.9 Source Diversity and Corroboration

Where consequence or uncertainty is high, Nexus should seek: independent corroboration; primary evidence; alternative interpretations; contradictory findings; affected stakeholder perspectives; domain expertise; jurisdiction-specific authority.

Source diversity does not mean treating all sources as equally credible. **Corroboration improves confidence only when sources are genuinely independent. Multiple reports derived from the same original claim do not constitute independent confirmation.**

## 5.10 Recency and Knowledge Expiry

**Knowledge does not remain current merely because it remains stored.**

Every material item should identify whether it is: timeless or principle-based; slow-changing; periodically changing; rapidly changing; event-specific; legally or commercially time-sensitive.

Where appropriate it must include: review date; expiry date; refresh trigger; responsible owner; authoritative replacement source.

Knowledge should be reviewed when: the legal or regulatory environment changes; technology changes materially; operating conditions change; contradictory evidence emerges; a decision produces unexpected results; stakeholders report changed circumstances; the source reaches its review date; doctrine or policy is amended; an agent identifies possible obsolescence; the cost of relying on outdated information becomes material.

**Expired knowledge may be retained for history but must not be treated as current.**

## 5.11 Contradiction Handling

**Contradictory evidence is a governance signal. It must not be hidden, averaged away, or resolved solely by selecting the most convenient source.**

When material contradiction exists, Nexus must: (1) identify the exact claims in conflict; (2) compare definitions and scope; (3) examine dates and operating contexts; (4) assess source reliability; (5) identify whether both claims may be conditionally true; (6) record unresolved uncertainty; (7) determine whether the contradiction affects active decisions; (8) escalate where consequence is material; (9) define the evidence required to resolve it; (10) preserve the disagreement for future review.

**A contested knowledge item must remain visibly contested until authorised resolution.**

## 5.12 Fact, Interpretation and Decision Separation

Nexus must distinguish: **what was observed; what the evidence suggests; what the organisation believes; what the organisation decided; what action was authorised.**

A governance decision may remain valid even when evidence is incomplete, provided uncertainty and authority were correctly handled.

**A decision does not transform an assumption into fact. An organisational belief does not become universal truth merely because it is constitutionally adopted.**

## 5.13 Doctrine Creation

Doctrine may originate from: constitutional principles; governance decisions; repeated operational experience; validated project lessons; risk reviews; audits; stakeholder outcomes; external research; agent-generated proposals; failure analysis; successful experimentation.

Doctrine may be adopted only when: its purpose is clear; its constitutional basis is identified; supporting evidence is documented; scope is defined; alternatives and trade-offs are understood; conflicts with existing doctrine are resolved; an owner is assigned; review conditions are established; appropriate authority approves it.

**Doctrine must not emerge accidentally through repeated practice.**

## 5.14 Doctrine Inheritance

**All projects, agents, and organisational systems must inherit relevant doctrine at initiation and at material transition points.**

Inheritance must identify: applicable constitutional principles; governance authority; domain doctrine; mandatory policies; active constraints; known exceptions; unresolved risks; current authoritative versions.

Projects may extend doctrine for local circumstances. **They may not silently contradict or override higher-order doctrine.** Where local requirements conflict, the conflict must be escalated and resolved through governance.

## 5.15 Doctrine Scope

Every doctrine item must define: title; identifier; purpose; constitutional basis; scope; audience; authority; mandatory and advisory components; exceptions; related doctrine; supporting evidence; owner; effective date; review date; version; superseded material.

Doctrine should be specific enough to guide consistent action but not so rigid that it prevents justified judgement.

## 5.16 Doctrine Conflict Resolution

Priority: (1) constitutional authority; (2) governance authority; (3) specificity to the current matter; (4) jurisdictional requirement; (5) recency of authorised adoption; (6) explicit exception; (7) materiality and risk.

Higher-order doctrine prevails unless a valid authorised exception exists. Conflicts must be recorded and corrected.

**Agents must not choose between conflicting doctrine without disclosing the conflict.**

## 5.17 Institutional Memory

Institutional memory is the curated record of what Nexus has learned, decided, attempted, rejected, changed, and preserved.

Its purpose is to prevent: repeated mistakes; loss of reasoning; dependence on individual memory; rediscovery of settled questions; doctrine fragmentation; contradictory project decisions; **silent loss of founder intent**; repeated research without cumulative learning.

**The purpose is not to store everything forever.** The purpose is to retain what the organisation may need to understand itself, make better decisions, or protect its stakeholders.

## 5.18 Curated Memory Standard

**Retain** when it: explains a material decision; establishes doctrine; records a constitutional interpretation; prevents a repeated failure; contains reusable evidence; demonstrates a successful method; **identifies a rejected alternative and why it was rejected**; preserves stakeholder context; supports legal, ethical, or governance accountability; protects continuity; reveals organisational evolution; may materially influence future decisions.

**Reduce, archive, or delete** when it: duplicates an authoritative record; has no plausible future value; creates privacy or security risk without sufficient justification; is misleading without its missing context; is temporary and its purpose has expired; has been lawfully scheduled for disposal; obstructs retrieval through unnecessary volume.

Retention must balance memory, privacy, security, cost, and usefulness.

## 5.19 Knowledge Ownership and Stewardship

Every material knowledge domain must have an accountable steward, responsible for: accuracy; structure; provenance; access; review; version control; contradiction handling; archival and retirement; inheritance; privacy and security; usability; continuity.

**Ownership does not create personal control over organisational knowledge.** Knowledge created through organisational activity is governed as an organisational asset, subject to legal, ethical, contractual, and privacy obligations.

## 5.20 Knowledge Access

Access must follow: legitimate purpose; least privilege; privacy; confidentiality; security; contractual obligations; legal requirements; stakeholder consent where applicable; constitutional need.

**Access restrictions must not be used to: protect personal power; conceal poor decisions; manufacture dependency; prevent appropriate challenge; obstruct governance; preserve outdated doctrine.**

Transparency does not require universal access. It requires that restrictions be justified, governed, and reviewable.

## 5.21 Knowledge Security and Integrity

Knowledge systems must protect against: unauthorised access; deletion; corruption; **silent modification**; misclassification; loss of provenance; data leakage; **malicious instruction**; accidental disclosure; version confusion; **fabricated records**; **unauthorised doctrine insertion**; **context contamination.**

Material knowledge changes should be attributable and recoverable.

**Where integrity cannot be assured, the affected knowledge must be isolated, validated, or downgraded before use.**

## 5.22 Knowledge Privacy

**Nexus must not collect information merely because it is technically possible.**

Personal, sensitive, confidential, or commercially restricted information must be: collected for a legitimate purpose; limited to what is necessary; protected appropriately; retained only as long as justified; accessed only by authorised parties; corrected where inaccurate; deleted or de-identified where required; **excluded from agent context where unnecessary.**

**The value of organisational memory must never be used to justify unlimited surveillance or permanent retention.**

## 5.23 Knowledge Retrieval

Retrieval systems should support: semantic search; keyword search; identifiers; project and domain classification; constitutional traceability; authority filters; version filters; status filters; date and recency; evidence quality; ownership; related decisions; related doctrine; **contradiction alerts.**

Retrieved information must show enough metadata for the user or agent to judge whether it is appropriate to rely upon.

**Relevance ranking must not hide authoritative material merely because less reliable content is more recent, popular, or linguistically similar.**

## 5.24 Single Source of Truth

A single source of truth means: one designated authoritative record; controlled versions; visible status; known ownership; governed amendment; links from derivative records; preservation of previous versions.

**It does not mean that all information must exist in one physical system.** Multiple systems may hold knowledge, provided authority and provenance remain unambiguous.

**Copies, summaries, and compressed versions must identify their authoritative source.**

## 5.25 Version Control

Version records should include: version identifier; date; author or agent; approver; change summary; rationale; evidence; affected dependencies; migration requirements; superseded version; rollback pathway where applicable.

**Silent overwriting is prohibited for constitutional, governance, doctrine, decision, and material project knowledge.**

Corrections must preserve the existence of the previous record where accountability requires it.

## 5.26 Knowledge Change Classification

**Editorial** — spelling, formatting, readability, or structure; no change of meaning.
**Clarifying** — makes existing meaning more explicit; no change to authority or intent.
**Corrective** — repairs an error.
**Additive** — new information without replacing existing meaning.
**Interpretive** — changes or extends how knowledge is understood.
**Substantive** — changes requirements, authority, scope, obligations, or expected behaviour.
**Constitutional** — changes founding meaning, constitutional authority, or constitutional standards.

The required approval increases with the significance of the change.

**An editorial label must not be used to conceal a substantive change.**

## 5.27 Knowledge Validation

Material knowledge should be tested for: accuracy; completeness; provenance; relevance; recency; internal consistency; external consistency; constitutional alignment; stakeholder appropriateness; privacy and security; known contradictions; actionability; **compression resilience.**

Validation may include: source comparison; expert review; operational testing; stakeholder verification; data reconciliation; **adversarial challenge**; scenario testing; reproduction of findings; **agent interpretation testing.**

**Validation must distinguish between confirming that a record is accurate and confirming that the conclusion drawn from it is justified.**

## 5.28 Knowledge Gaps

A knowledge gap must be recorded when information necessary for responsible action is: missing; unreliable; outdated; inaccessible; contradictory; insufficiently specific; outside current expertise; legally restricted; impossible to obtain within the decision window.

A gap record should identify: the unanswered question; why it matters; consequence of remaining uncertain; evidence required; owner; research pathway; decision deadline; interim assumption, if authorised; escalation threshold.

**Unknowns must remain visible. They must not be filled with plausible invention.**

## 5.29 Research Governance

A material research package should identify: research question; constitutional relevance; scope; decision supported; source strategy; recency requirements; jurisdiction; known assumptions; evidence standard; contradictions; limitations; findings; confidence; recommended next action.

Research should stop when sufficient evidence exists for the consequence and reversibility of the decision.

**Research must not continue indefinitely to avoid responsibility. Equally, urgency must not be used to justify avoidable ignorance.**

## 5.30 Artificial Intelligence and Knowledge Creation

**AI-generated content is not self-validating.**

AI may: locate information; summarise; classify; compare; translate; identify relationships; detect possible contradictions; prepare research; recommend knowledge links; propose doctrine; generate hypotheses; assist retrieval.

AI-generated knowledge must disclose: that AI materially contributed; original sources where applicable; level of human or system validation; uncertainty; transformations performed; whether the output contains inference.

**Agents must not fabricate: sources; quotations; decisions; approvals; project history; stakeholder statements; system records; completed work; certainty.**

**Where an AI-generated summary conflicts with the authoritative source, the authoritative source governs.**

## 5.31 Agent Memory

Agents may retain or retrieve information only where it supports: continuity; personalisation; authorised work; organisational learning; safety; governance; constitutional alignment.

Agent memory must distinguish: user-provided facts; organisational records; inferred preferences; temporary session context; durable authorised memory; unverified interpretation.

**Agents must not convert an inference into a permanent fact.** Sensitive information must not be retained without a legitimate and authorised basis.

## 5.32 Agent Handoff Standard

Every material handoff between agents, systems, projects, or sessions should preserve: objective; constitutional purpose; current status; decisions made; authority granted; actions completed; evidence; assumptions; unresolved questions; risks; next authorised action; relevant doctrine; authoritative source locations.

A handoff must distinguish: **completed; approved; proposed; pending; blocked; inferred; unknown.**

**The receiving agent must validate the handoff before relying upon it for material action.**

## 5.33 Context Compression

Compression **must preserve**: constitutional intent; authority; commitments; decisions; provenance; unresolved risk; active constraints; material evidence; dissent; next actions; status.

Compression **may remove**: repetition; superseded working detail; low-value dialogue; intermediate drafts; resolved questions; duplicated sources; temporary formatting.

Compression **must not**: erase uncertainty; **convert proposals into approvals**; remove dissent; remove the reason behind a material decision; **merge conflicting claims into false agreement**; remove conditions attached to authority; **represent incomplete work as complete**; alter constitutional language; discard provenance required for verification.

## 5.34 Context Compressor Constitutional Requirements

Any context compressor, including `context_compressor.py` or an equivalent system, must:

1. identify the constitutional layer relevant to the context;
2. preserve the Constitutional Fingerprint;
3. preserve decision authority and non-delegables;
4. retain the current authoritative status of each material item;
5. retain evidence provenance or pointers to authoritative sources;
6. retain unresolved contradictions and uncertainty;
7. preserve conditions, dependencies, and approval boundaries;
8. distinguish historical facts from current instructions;
9. distinguish proposed work from completed work;
10. record the compression date and method;
11. identify the source material compressed;
12. provide a route to recover additional detail;
13. **avoid generating missing facts**;
14. flag possible meaning loss;
15. undergo validation before use in material reasoning.

**A compressed context is a derivative knowledge product. It does not replace the authoritative source unless governance explicitly designates it as authoritative.**

## 5.35 Compression Integrity Test

A compressed context passes when an authorised reviewer or agent can correctly determine: why the work exists; what constitutional principles apply; what has been decided; what remains undecided; who holds authority; what actions are permitted; what evidence supports the current position; what uncertainty remains; what risks exist; what should happen next.

**Compression fails when a reasonable agent could take materially different action because essential context was removed or distorted.**

## 5.36 Organisational Knowledge Graph

Nexus should progressively maintain relationships between: constitutional principles; governance decisions; doctrine; policies; evidence; projects; stakeholders; agents; outcomes; risks; amendments; lessons; knowledge gaps.

**The purpose is not technical complexity.** Its purpose is to answer: Why does this rule exist? Which decisions depend on this evidence? Which projects inherit this doctrine? What changes if this assumption is false? Where has this problem occurred before? What knowledge is at risk of becoming outdated? What lessons should transfer across the ecosystem? Which constitutional principle is being advanced or threatened?

## 5.37 Continuous Organisational Cognition

Nexus may continuously: observe authorised signals; identify emerging knowledge; detect contradictions; monitor expiry; connect related lessons; identify doctrine gaps; prepare research; surface risks; recommend review; stage knowledge updates.

**Continuous cognition does not grant continuous authority.** The system may prepare, classify, and recommend. **It may not silently declare contested information authoritative, amend doctrine, or execute material changes without approval.**

## 5.38 Knowledge Review Rhythm

**Continuous** — capture material evidence; preserve provenance; identify contradictions; monitor security and integrity; record decisions and outcomes; surface knowledge gaps.
**Monthly** — review high-impact knowledge changes; inspect expiring evidence; reconcile contradictions affecting active work; validate agent-generated knowledge proposals; examine retrieval failures.
**Quarterly** — doctrine relevance review; knowledge reuse assessment; project lesson transfer; duplication and fragmentation review; institutional memory health check.
**Annually** — authoritative knowledge audit; doctrine inheritance audit; retention and deletion review; source reliability review; knowledge security review; context-compression integrity testing; constitutional knowledge continuity review.
**Every Three to Five Years** — deep institutional memory review; knowledge architecture evaluation; examination of whether inherited doctrine still serves constitutional purpose; review of historical knowledge required to preserve organisational identity.

## 5.39 Knowledge Quality Indicators

Percentage of material knowledge with provenance; percentage with current review status; retrieval success; time to find authoritative information; unresolved contradictions; outdated evidence in active use; duplicate or competing doctrine; agent reliance on unverified material; context-compression failures; repeated mistakes caused by inaccessible knowledge; lessons reused across projects; knowledge gaps affecting material decisions; **silent edit incidents**; **institutional memory lost during handoff**; stakeholder corrections; doctrine adoption and retirement quality.

**These indicators diagnose knowledge-system performance. They do not replace judgement.**

## 5.40 Failure Modes

This framework is designed to prevent: knowledge fragmentation; **organisational amnesia**; false certainty; outdated evidence guiding current decisions; **agent hallucination becoming institutional memory**; unsupported doctrine; duplicated research; contradictory project rules; inaccessible authoritative knowledge; **founder intent being diluted through summarisation**; silent rewriting; uncontrolled data accumulation; privacy violations; information overload; dependency on individual memory; **context compression altering authority**; **rejected ideas returning without their previous analysis**; **working assumptions becoming permanent truth.**

## 5.41 Constitutional Knowledge Register

Each entry: identifier; title; knowledge type; authority level; status; scope; owner; source; provenance; evidence quality; effective date; review or expiry date; related doctrine; related decisions; contradictions; privacy classification; access classification; authoritative location; compressed fingerprint; superseded versions; retention requirement.

## 5.42 Institutional Memory Record

Material lessons: situation; original objective; context; action taken; evidence available at the time; outcome; success or failure; unexpected consequences; stakeholder impact; constitutional implications; lesson; doctrine affected; future warning signs; reusable recommendations; related records.

**The record should explain not only what happened, but why the lesson matters.**

## 5.43 Knowledge Decision Standard

Before relying on a material knowledge item: (1) What is the claim? (2) What type of knowledge is it? (3) What supports it? (4) How reliable is the source? (5) How current is it? (6) What limitations apply? (7) Is it contested? (8) What constitutional or operational decision depends on it? (9) What happens if it is wrong? (10) Who has authority to validate or adopt it?

If these cannot be answered, reliance must be proportionate to the uncertainty and consequence.

## 5.44 Constitutional Knowledge Fingerprint

> Knowledge is an organisational asset, not an accumulation of files.
> Preserve constitutional meaning and decision authority.
> Distinguish fact, evidence, inference, assumption, belief, and decision.
> Record provenance, status, confidence, ownership, and review conditions.
> Prefer authoritative sources while preserving credible contradiction.
> Adopt doctrine deliberately; never allow repetition to create silent policy.
> Transfer lessons across projects.
> Retain what improves future judgement; remove what creates unjustified risk or noise.
> Allow agents to organise and propose knowledge, but not invent authority or certainty.
> Compress detail without compressing truth.
> Learn continuously without rewriting the constitutional core.

## 5.45 Constitutional Principles

> Nexus must never forget why it knows what it knows.

> Information becomes institutional knowledge only when its source, meaning, authority, and limitations are preserved.

> Memory must serve future judgement, not merely preserve the past.

> The organisation must remain capable of changing its conclusions without losing the record of how and why they changed.

> Compression may reduce volume. It must never reduce constitutional integrity.

## 5.46 Success Standard for This Framework

This framework succeeds when: authorised people and agents can locate trustworthy knowledge when needed; material claims remain traceable to evidence; current and historical knowledge are clearly distinguished; doctrine is inherited consistently; contradictory evidence is visible and governed; project learning compounds across the ecosystem; institutional knowledge does not depend on individual memory; outdated information is identified before causing material harm; AI-generated content does not become authoritative without validation; context compression preserves meaning, authority, status, and risk; privacy and security remain proportional to purpose; founder intent survives organisational growth and technical change; **Nexus becomes more intelligent without becoming less accountable.**
